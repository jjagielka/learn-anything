# The six methods — full playbooks

Each method below has: **when to use**, the **procedure** to follow, the
**output format** to produce, and the **principle** behind it (say the principle
to the learner once — it earns their buy-in). Run methods through the coaching
lens in `SKILL.md`: goal-first, honest, momentum over completeness.

Throughout, `[topic]` = what they're learning, `[goal]` = what they want to do
with it. Always re-derive content from `[goal]`, not from a generic syllabus.

---

## §1 — Learn [topic] in 20 hours (the 80/20 plan)

**When:** they want a plan, a course structure, or "learn X in N hours."

**Procedure:**
1. Identify the **20% of concepts that drive 80% of real-world results** for
   *their goal*. Most subjects have only 3–4 ideas that unlock everything else —
   name them explicitly. Finding these early saves weeks of wandering.
2. Build a **10-session plan, 2 hours each** (scale to their time budget). For
   each session: a focused objective, the single best resource for it (see §5),
   and what they'll be able to do by the end.
3. Front-load the 3–4 core ideas into the earliest sessions. Later sessions
   apply and extend.
4. End every session with a **5-question review** (see §3).

**Output format:** a numbered session table — `# · Focus · Resource · You'll be
able to…` — preceded by the 3–4 core ideas called out separately. Keep it
scannable.

**Principle:** *Most subjects have 3–4 ideas that unlock everything else.
Finding them first is the whole game.*

---

## §2 — One-page cheat sheet

**When:** they want a summary, a reference, or a review aid.

**Procedure:**
1. Compress `[topic]` (or the session's material) onto **a single page**.
2. Use **bullet points, labeled diagrams (ASCII/mermaid is fine), and concrete
   examples** — not paragraphs. Structure over prose.
3. Optimize it for a **5-minute review the night before they need it**: group by
   how they'll actually reach for it, put the highest-leverage items on top.
4. If a log exists, this is a **living document** — grow it session by session
   rather than regenerating from scratch.

**Output format:** one page. Sections with headers, tight bullets, a diagram or
two, worked examples. Nothing that wouldn't survive a 5-minute skim.

**Principle:** *Your brain remembers structure better than sentences. Give it a
map, not a wall of text.*

---

## §3 — Quiz me until I break

**When:** they just studied something, ask to be tested, or a session is ending.

**Procedure:**
1. Ask **10 questions that get progressively harder** — start easy (recall), end
   at expert level (apply, connect, edge cases). Ask them one or a few at a time;
   wait for answers. Don't reveal answers early.
2. After each answer: **grade it**, **name the specific gap**, and **re-explain
   only what they missed** — briefly, then move on.
3. Push difficulty until they **break** — the first question they can't do marks
   the exact frontier of their knowledge. That's the target, not a failure.
4. Log every miss under **Open gaps** and re-surface it in a later session
   (spacing).

**Output format:** conversational, one/few questions at a time. End with a short
verdict: where they're solid, where they broke, what to drill next.

**Principle:** *Passive reading feels like learning. Active retrieval actually is
learning.*

---

## §4 — Build a learning ladder

**When:** they want a roadmap, levels, or "how do I go from beginner to good."

**Procedure:**
1. Break `[topic]` into **5 difficulty levels**, complete beginner → confident
   practitioner.
2. For each level define **what mastery looks like** (an observable capability,
   not a vibe).
3. Give each level **one milestone** (proof they've arrived) and **one hands-on
   exercise** (how they get there). Doing, not reading.
4. Place the learner on the ladder now, and point at the next rung only — don't
   let them skip levels.

**Output format:** 5 levels, each with `Mastery looks like / Milestone /
Exercise`. Mark the learner's current position.

**Principle:** *Most people skip levels and wonder why nothing sticks. The ladder
fixes that.*

---

## §5 — Find the signal in the noise

**When:** they ask what to read/watch/use, or before building the §1 plan.

**Procedure:**
1. List the **5 highest-leverage resources** for learning `[topic]` fast —
   books, videos, courses, docs, or people.
2. For each: **what makes it worth their time** and **which type of learner it
   suits** (beginner vs rusty, reader vs hands-on, theory vs application).
3. Bias hard toward their `[goal]` and starting level. One perfect resource beats
   ten decent ones.
4. Flag the single best **first** resource explicitly.

**Output format:** 5 entries — `Resource · Why it's worth it · Best for`. Star
the recommended starting point.

**Principle:** *There are 1,000 ways to learn anything and most are a waste.
Curation is leverage.*

---

## §6 — The Feynman loop

**When:** they want to check real understanding, or say "explain like I'm 12."

**Procedure:**
1. **Explain `[topic]` like they're 12** — plain words, one clear analogy, no
   jargon.
2. Ask them to **explain it back in their own words.**
3. Find **every gap, fuzzy spot, or wrong turn** in their explanation.
4. **Re-teach only what they got wrong** — then ask them to explain that part
   again.
5. **Repeat until their explanation is clean.** Then declare it: they own this
   concept now.

**Output format:** a loop. Your simple explanation → their turn → your targeted
corrections → their retry. Keep cycling the rough parts only.

**Principle:** *If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet. This
loop exposes the gap fast — and closes it.*

---

## Combining methods (the default journey)

Signal (§5) picks the material → 20-Hour Plan (§1) structures it → Cheat Sheet
(§2) becomes the living map → Ladder (§4) sets the progression → Quiz (§3) +
Feynman (§6) drive retrieval every session. See `SKILL.md` Steps 0–4 for the full
orchestration and the progress log.
